## The Bottom Line

Virginia SB 727 is not the assault weapons bill everyone is watching. It bans carrying them statewide, applies to unloaded guns, covers a huge swath of common pistols, and offers zero grandfather protection. Normal gun owners who follow every other law will become criminals under this bill if the governor does not veto it by April 13.

## The bill nobody's talking about

Virginia's 2026 legislative session produced a wave of gun control. Fifteen-plus anti-gun bills passed in 60 days, and the headline item, SB 749, the assault weapons sales and transfer ban, got most of the attention. That bill came with a grandfather clause for existing owners. It's bad law, but legislators understood that confiscating firearms people already own would draw immediate constitutional challenge.

SB 727 is more surgical and far more dangerous for the average Virginia gun owner. It doesn't ban possession. It bans carrying. And that distinction is how the drafters of this bill avoided writing a grandfather clause at all.

The legal logic works like this: if you can keep the gun at home but cannot carry it anywhere in public, you still technically "own" it. Legislators can claim they're not confiscating anything. But a firearm you cannot transport to a range, carry for self-defense, or move between locations is not a practical exercise of a constitutional right. It's a decoration.

## What the bill actually covers

SB 727 rewrites the definition of "assault firearm" and bans carrying any qualifying firearm on any public street, sidewalk, road, park, or public right-of-way across the entire Commonwealth. Previously this kind of restriction applied only in 13 localities and only to loaded firearms. The enrolled bill removes both limitations.

The rifle definitions capture most modern sporting rifles. Any semi-automatic center-fire rifle with a detachable magazine and a single qualifying feature, including a collapsible stock, pistol grip, thumbhole stock, threaded barrel, or forward grip, is prohibited. Notably, the bill also captures firearms that are merely capable of accepting a magazine over 20 rounds, regardless of what magazine is actually in the gun when you're carrying it. If a manufacturer somewhere in the world produces a 21-round magazine compatible with your rifle, your rifle qualifies as an assault firearm under this law.

The shotgun definitions are even more aggressive. A semi-automatic shotgun with a detachable magazine is banned. Any shotgun, semi-automatic or not, with a fixed magazine capacity over seven rounds qualifies. Seven rounds. A standard tube-fed 12-gauge with eight shells in the magazine is an assault firearm under SB 727.

The pistol section is where this gets particularly consequential. A semi-automatic center-fire pistol with a detachable magazine, which describes the overwhelming majority of modern handguns, qualifies as an assault firearm if it has any one of the following: a second hand grip or forward grip, a magazine that inserts outside the pistol grip, a barrel shroud, or a threaded barrel. AR-platform pistols with arm braces are explicitly captured. The arm brace itself, including a buffer tube or any part that protrudes horizontally behind the pistol grip, is listed as a qualifying feature.

Your concealed handgun permit protects you from none of this. The bill carves out exceptions for law enforcement, military personnel on duty, and shooters at established ranges. Permit holders are not in the exceptions list.

## No compliance path, no grace period

This is the detail that most coverage has missed. SB 749, the sales ban, grandfathers everything you already own. Legally purchased assault weapons before July 1 are yours to keep. SB 727 includes no such accommodation. There is no registration option, no phase-in period, no compliance path whatsoever.

If this bill becomes law, a Virginia resident who legally purchased a pistol with a threaded barrel last month will be committing a Class 1 misdemeanor the moment they carry it on a public sidewalk, whether or not it's loaded. The penalty is up to 12 months in jail and up to $2,500 in fines per offense. The court has discretion to impose both.

In my view, the absence of a grandfather clause is not an oversight. It's the design. Gun control advocates know that a carry ban is legally cleaner than a possession ban because it doesn't require them to compensate owners or defend against takings claims. The firearm is still technically yours. You just can't do anything with it.

## The constitutional picture

The Supreme Court's 2022 decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** established that the government must demonstrate a historical tradition of analogous firearm regulation to justify modern restrictions. The standard applies to laws that burden the right to keep and bear arms for self-defense.

A statewide carry ban on semi-automatic pistols with threaded barrels or detachable magazines reaching outside the grip is a very difficult law to defend under that framework. The features being targeted are cosmetic or ergonomic. They don't change how the firearm functions in any way that tracks with 18th or 19th century regulatory concerns.

The Fourth Circuit, which covers Virginia, has not always been receptive to Second Amendment challenges. But post-**Bruen** litigation has forced even unfriendly circuits to reckon with the historical analysis standard in ways they didn't before. A blanket carry ban applied to firearms that millions of Americans own for self-defense, with no provision for existing owners, is the kind of law that invites serious challenge.

The question is whether a challenge gets filed fast enough to block enforcement before July 1.

## Who is actually affected

Walk through the practical implications. A Virginian who owns a Ruger 10/22 with a folding stock, kept legally at home for years, takes it to the range and becomes a criminal in transit if SB 727 takes effect without modification. A competition shooter whose pistol has a magazine that seats outside the grip, a common configuration, cannot transport that firearm to a match. Someone driving across Virginia with an unloaded AR-pattern pistol in a locked case, on the way to a lawful destination, is committing a Class 1 misdemeanor regardless of the lock, the case, or the lack of ammunition.

Governor Spanberger has until April 13. She has publicly committed to signing the high-profile SB 749 assault weapons sales ban. Her position on SB 727 has been less explicit, but she came into office on a gun control platform and has shown no indication she views these bills as overreach.

If she signs or allows SB 727 to take effect without a veto, Virginia will be the only state in the country with a statewide carry ban on assault firearms that includes no grandfather protection and no registration pathway. Not California. Not New York. Not Massachusetts. Virginia.

That is where this is heading.
