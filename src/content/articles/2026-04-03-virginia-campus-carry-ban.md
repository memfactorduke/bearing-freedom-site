## The Bottom Line

Virginia HB626 strips concealed handgun permit holders of their right to carry in any building on a public college campus, converting a lawful act into a Class 1 misdemeanor punishable by up to a year in jail. One delegate defending the bill cited a Holocaust survivor's death at Virginia Tech as a reason people shouldn't be allowed to carry. That tells you everything you need to know about the intent behind this law.

## What the bill actually does

Virginia House Bill 626 amends the state statute governing firearms in Commonwealth-owned buildings. Under current law, concealed handgun permit holders can carry on public university campuses unless the institution has adopted a policy restricting it. That institutional opt-out is imperfect from a rights standpoint, but it at least preserves the legal status of carrying. HB626 eliminates the blanket higher education exemption entirely.

After July 1, 2026, if this bill becomes law, your Virginia concealed handgun permit, the one the state already vetted you for, means nothing inside any campus building. Classrooms, libraries, dormitories, student centers, offices, labs: off-limits. The penalty for violation is a Class 1 misdemeanor. That's up to 12 months in jail and a substantial fine. It goes on your record and can affect your employment. Today you can lawfully carry. In three months you could go to jail for the same act.

The practical impact extends well beyond students and faculty who spend most of their time on campus. Consider a physician who works in a university research lab. Their daily commute takes them from home, to campus, to a grocery store, to their child's school. Under HB626, that person faces an impossible choice: leave their firearm at home every day, or figure out where in their commute to safely secure it before and after campus hours. That's not a theoretical edge case. That's the routine of a lawful gun owner whose workplace happens to fall under a university's umbrella.

## The Liviu Librescu argument

Delegate Maguire, arguing against an exception for ROTC cadets during floor debate, invoked the name of Liviu Librescu to make the case that armed resistance isn't necessary on a campus.

Librescu was a Romanian-American engineering professor at Virginia Tech. He survived a Jewish ghetto as a child, survived Nicolae Ceaușescu's totalitarian regime as a dissident academic, survived rejection by the Soviet bloc's academic establishment before eventually reaching the United States. On April 16, 2007, during the [Virginia Tech shooting](https://www.weremember.vt.edu/biographies/librescu.html), he held the door to his classroom shut with his body while the gunman attempted to enter, allowing most of his students to escape through the windows before he was shot and killed. He was 76 years old. The shooting happened to fall on Yom HaShoah, Holocaust Remembrance Day.

Delegate Maguire's argument was that because Librescu managed to protect his students without a firearm, this demonstrates that people don't need guns to fight back. I want to be precise about what this argument requires you to believe. It requires you to believe that a 76-year-old man dying while using his body as a door barricade represents an acceptable, even admirable, alternative to having the means to defend yourself. It requires you to celebrate an unarmed person's death as evidence that an armed person would have been unnecessary.

The delegate is right that Librescu's sacrifice was heroic. He is wrong to present it as a policy argument. Heroism in a terrible situation does not prove that the situation was acceptable. Librescu was disarmed by the state's prohibition on campus carry. He did the most he could do with what he had. If he had been permitted to carry, we cannot know what would have happened, but we know he would have had options beyond absorbing bullets.

## The claim that campus carry increases danger

Proponents of bills like HB626 frequently cite the argument that more guns on campus create more risk. The [research on this is genuinely contested](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11874404/), and I won't pretend otherwise. But a few things are clear.

Virginia's CHP process is not trivial. Applicants undergo a background check, and courts verify that the applicant is not prohibited from possessing a firearm. The people who would be disarmed by HB626 are not random members of the public who stumbled into possession of a handgun. They are people the state of Virginia has already cleared to carry in grocery stores, movie theaters, shopping malls, and every other public space. The argument that this same population becomes suddenly dangerous the moment they step onto a university quad requires some explanation that supporters of HB626 haven't provided.

Twenty-three states [currently permit campus carry](https://www.campussafetymagazine.com/news/list-of-states-that-allow-concealed-carry-guns-on-campus/40287/) in some form. The predictions of widespread violence that accompanied the expansion of campus carry laws have not materialized into a measurable pattern. That's not a definitive proof that campus carry is safe, but it does undercut the catastrophizing that drives legislation like HB626.

## The person most harmed by this law

Something that often gets overlooked in these debates is who actually carries a concealed firearm on campus under current Virginia law. It isn't undergraduates. The typical campus carry permit holder is an adult graduate student, a faculty or staff member, or a researcher, someone with a fixed professional presence on campus who made a considered decision to carry for self-protection and went through the legal process to do so. These are not impulsive actors. They are exactly the kind of responsible gun owners the permitting process is designed to identify.

HB626 tells those people that their judgment doesn't matter and their permit doesn't count as long as they're within the boundaries of a state university. It does this while doing nothing to prevent a person with violent intent from bringing a weapon onto campus in violation of the law. That's the consistent failure of gun-free zone policies: they bind the people who follow rules and do nothing about the people who don't.

## What July 1 means

The bill heads to Governor Spanberger's desk with an April 13th deadline. If she signs it or simply doesn't veto it, Virginia CHP holders who carry on public college campuses face a Class 1 misdemeanor charge starting July 1st. I think most people in that situation will adjust their behavior to avoid prosecution, which is the intended outcome. The goal is not to punish gun owners. The goal is to disarm them in spaces where they would otherwise have a legal right to carry.

In my view, invoking Liviu Librescu's death as a reason not to allow self-defense removed any pretense that this legislation is about safety. A man survived the Holocaust, survived communist Romania, came to America, and was shot to death in a gun-free building on a Virginia campus. The lesson his story teaches about government-mandated disarmament is the opposite of the one Delegate Maguire drew from it.
