## Volume as a weapon

When Virginia Democrats introduced roughly 58 gun-related measures in the 2026 legislative session, the immediate instinct for most people was to treat it as a policy debate. Count the votes, track the amendments, figure out which ones will pass. That instinct is understandable, but it misses the point. The volume itself is the tactic.

Gun rights advocates, journalists, and ordinary citizens have a finite amount of attention. Running 58 bills simultaneously means no single bill receives the sustained scrutiny it would if introduced alone. An assault weapons ban introduced as a standalone measure generates weeks of coverage, public hearings, floor debate, and organized opposition. That same ban buried in a package of 57 other measures gets a fraction of that attention. Opponents are forced to react to everything at once, which means they respond to nothing effectively. Virginia Democrats, backed by well-funded national organizations like Moms Demand Action and Everytown, understand this completely.

By the time the General Assembly adjourned on March 14, 2026, approximately 25 gun control measures had cleared both chambers and landed on Governor Abigail Spanberger's desk, with a signing deadline of April 13th. The Virginia Citizens Defense League tracked the full package, and the numbers are staggering. Of the 58 bills, about 20 were killed outright. Nearly everything else either passed or is on a path to becoming law without requiring the governor's signature.

## The specific damage

It's worth looking at what actually cleared this session rather than treating it as an abstraction.

**SB 115** strips concealed carry reciprocity with other states by handing authority to the state police superintendent to determine whether any given state's permitting regime is "substantially similar" to Virginia's. In practice this is a carry ban for the overwhelming majority of out-of-state permit holders. Anyone living near Virginia's borders who routinely carries legally in the state is now at serious legal risk. That includes active duty military from neighboring states, travelers, and the many residents of states like North Carolina and Tennessee whose permits will simply not be recognized under the new standard.

**SB 643 and HB 1525** raise the purchase age for handguns and "assault firearms" to 21, with no grandfather clause for young adults who already lawfully own such firearms. An 18-year-old who legally bought a handgun last year would be in violation. That is not an oversight. Making existing lawful ownership retroactively criminal is a deliberate choice, and it tells you something important about the underlying philosophy.

**SB 323** targets so-called ghost guns by prohibiting the manufacture of firearms without serial numbers, as well as possession and transfer of unserialized firearms. Whatever one thinks about that particular policy, it's worth noting that most ghost guns used in crimes were not assembled by hobbyists working from 80-percent lowers. They're converted or stolen commercial firearms. The actual effect of this bill falls almost entirely on law-abiding people who were building legal firearms for personal use.

**SB 364** establishes a state office whose function is to fund "gun violence prevention" organizations and programs. This is taxpayer money flowing to advocacy groups whose explicit purpose is to generate more gun control legislation. The Virginia Department of Criminal Justice Services already administers a Firearm Violence Intervention and Prevention grant program. SB 364 expands and formalizes that pipeline. Gun owners in Virginia will be taxed to fund the organizations lobbying against their rights.

**HB 969** requires the Department of Criminal Justice Services to convene a working group to produce policy and legislative recommendations on "firearm violence." The working group's composition includes "firearm safety advocates," which is standard legislative language for anti-gun organizations, alongside state officials and legislators. The output will be another round of restrictions. The machine is being institutionalized.

**HB 1524**, which was still in conference committee as of mid-March, would prohibit carrying broadly defined "assault firearms" in public spaces. The definition is wide enough to capture commonly carried pistols, and the vagueness is intentional. Legislators correcting each other on the floor about whether to say "semi-automatic pistol" or "assault pistol" is not a sign of careful draftsmanship. It's a sign that the categories are being invented as needed.

## What the zone-flooding strategy actually costs

There is a real human cost to this kind of legislative volume that gets lost in the abstraction of bill numbers and conference committees. Concealed carry holders who have carried legally in Virginia for years now face potential felony exposure if they cross the border with a North Carolina or Tennessee permit. Young adults who followed the law and bought a handgun at 18 or 19 are suddenly in a gray area about whether they can keep it. FFLs and dealers are navigating an expanding web of storage requirements, civil liability exposure, and age restriction verification obligations, each with its own penalties.

The people who experience none of this are the people committing crimes. The Virginia State Police have not proposed a mechanism by which any of these bills reduces the volume of illegally trafficked firearms moving through the commonwealth. They're not designed to. The entire burden falls on law-abiding gun owners, which is why the Virginia Citizens Defense League describes the session's output as turning "sportsmen into criminals."

## The national model

Virginia isn't an isolated case. It's a test of what a disciplined, well-funded gun control majority can accomplish in a single session when it controls the legislature and the governor's office. Organizations like Everytown have been explicit that Virginia's legislative outcomes are a template for other states. The coordination between the state and national level on this is not hidden. The same policy priorities, the same bill language, the same volume strategy, have appeared in Colorado, Washington, and Illinois in recent cycles.

In my view, the appropriate response to this isn't despair. About 20 of those 58 bills were killed, which means organized opposition had real effect. The assault weapons ban passed both chambers but still hasn't been signed as of this writing, and there's at least some uncertainty about what form of the carry restriction bill Spanberger will ultimately receive. The legislative session is over. The scoring phase is beginning.

What concerns me more than the bills themselves is the infrastructure being built. An office funded by state taxes whose purpose is to generate more gun control legislation. A working group of "firearm safety advocates" producing recommendations that become the next session's bills. Virginia isn't just passing gun control. It's building a permanent institutional capacity to keep passing it, session after session, until the opposition runs out of resources to fight back.

That's the part that doesn't make the headlines. And that's exactly where I think gun owners need to focus their attention.
