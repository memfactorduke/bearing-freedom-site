## The bottom line

Trump fired Pam Bondi on April 2, 2026, and named Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche as acting AG. For the Second Amendment, this is not a sideways move. It is an upgrade, and a meaningful one.

## How this went down

I want to be honest about how I saw this coming, because it matters for understanding the political machinery involved. On the morning of April 2nd, I was watching the Polymarket odds on Pam Bondi's departure. She had been sitting at around 14% to leave before May 1st. Then, in the span of a few hours, those odds jumped from 14% to 31%, then to 45%, then all the way to 76%. By the time I was recording my thoughts at 11:30 in the morning, those numbers had nearly doubled in under two hours.

Prediction markets do not move like that on rumor alone. [Polymarket](https://polymarket.com/) is not a Twitter poll. It is people putting real capital behind real beliefs, and sharp moves like this one happen when someone with insider knowledge decides to act on it. By 10 a.m., the odds had spiked again. The story broke shortly after: Trump was done with Bondi, and Blanche was stepping up.

The reasons given publicly center on Bondi's handling of the Jeffrey Epstein files and a broader frustration that she was not executing Trump's vision at the department. A source close to the White House told [NewsNation](https://www.newsnation.com/) that while Trump liked her personally, she had not delivered what he wanted. [CBS News confirmed](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/trump-pam-bondi-attorney-general/) that Blanche would take over as acting attorney general immediately. Bondi becomes the second Trump cabinet member fired this term, following Kristi Noem at Homeland Security in March.

## Bondi's Florida record is not a footnote

I have defended Bondi where defense was warranted. She put good people below her at DOJ, and the department's direction on guns improved considerably over the Biden years. That is real, and I am not going to pretend otherwise.

But Bondi's record as Florida attorney general from 2011 to 2019 is not something you can wave away, and Gun Owners of America made sure to say so publicly when she was nominated. [GOA's formal opposition piece](https://www.gunowners.org/na011025/) laid it out plainly: after the 2018 Parkland shooting, Bondi worked alongside then-Governor Rick Scott to pass the Marjory Stoneman Douglas High School Public Safety Act. That law created Florida's red flag "Risk Protection Order" system, raised the purchase age for all long guns to 21, and banned bump stocks statewide. Bondi did not just acquiesce to it. She championed it and then defended it in court against NRA legal challenges.

At her [Senate confirmation hearing](https://www.cnn.com/2025/02/04/politics/pam-bondi-confirmed-attorney-general/index.html), she told senators she stood by Florida's post-Parkland laws and was open to reviewing similar federal proposals. The Senate confirmed her 54-46 on February 4, 2025, with Democrat John Fetterman of Pennsylvania crossing the aisle to vote yes. GOA's objections did not carry the day, but they were accurate.

She was not the enemy. She was, at best, a competent administrator who happened to staff her department with people who actually believed in the Second Amendment. There is a real difference between those two things, and that difference is now sitting in the attorney general's chair.

## What Blanche has actually done

Todd Blanche is not an unknown quantity on guns. He spent his time as deputy AG showing up in places that matter to the gun community in ways that went well beyond photo opportunities.

He attended [SHOT Show 2026](https://www.breitbart.com/2nd-amendment/2026/01/22/exclusive-deputy-attorney-general-todd-blanche-describes-fight-protect-second-amendment-rights/) in Las Vegas and spent serious time on the floor with firearm industry members. He sat in on the ATF town hall session. He told Breitbart directly that anti-gun advocates are "crafty" and "creative" in their ongoing violations of the Second Amendment. That is the language of someone who has been paying attention. He also stated publicly that "this administration and Department of Justice will fiercely protect Second Amendment rights." That is the kind of quote that means something when it comes from the person who is now running the department.

At the SHOT Show ATF session, Blanche signaled movement on the federal firearms rights restoration program, which processes applications from people who have had their rights stripped under federal law and want them restored. That program was effectively frozen under Biden. Under Blanche, the indication was clear that it would move. [NRA-ILA covered the takeaways from that session](https://www.nraila.org/articles/20260126/second-amendment-momentum-quick-takeaways-from-shot-show) and confirmed Blanche was signaling additional legal pressure on states still defying *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022).

At [CPAC 2026](https://redstate.com/jenniferoo/2026/03/26/cpac-the-justice-league-restoring-the-constitution-with-deputy-attorney-general-todd-blanche-n2200654/), Blanche talked about restoring constitutional governance and real justice, not as talking points but in specific terms about what DOJ was doing and where it was going. The man has been consistent across venues where he did not have to show up at all.

## The Second Amendment infrastructure at DOJ is real

One of the things that does not get enough attention is what Harmeet Dhillon has been building inside the Civil Rights Division. As assistant attorney general for civil rights, she announced the creation of a dedicated [Second Amendment Section at DOJ's Civil Rights Division](https://justthenews.com/government/federal-agencies/harmeet-dhillon-announces-new-second-amendment-section-doj-civil-rights), the first of its kind. She has been explicit about the goal: get every state regulation inconsistent with *Heller* and *Bruen* struck down, settled, or withdrawn before she leaves office. She is [hiring specifically for this mission](https://www.americas1stfreedom.org/content/doj-civil-rights-division-s-new-second-amendment-section-hits-the-ground-running/).

The cases already filed reflect this. The Civil Rights Division went to the 7th Circuit to challenge Illinois' "assault weapons" ban. It filed an amicus in *Wolford v. Lopez* at the Supreme Court targeting Hawaii's carry restrictions. It sued the Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department in federal court for refusing to process carry permits in compliance with *Bruen*. It filed in *Rhode v. Bonta* at the Ninth Circuit challenging California's ammunition background check system. As of late March, the [Washington Times reported](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2026/mar/31/doj-arming-second-amendment-lawyers-take-tranche-state-gun-control/) that DOJ was staffing up to take on an additional wave of state gun control laws.

And on January 15, 2026, the Office of Legal Counsel issued a [landmark opinion](https://www.ammoland.com/2026/01/doj-declares-federal-ban-on-mailing-handguns-unconstitutional/) declaring 18 U.S.C. § 1715, the nearly century-old federal ban on mailing concealable firearms, unconstitutional under *Bruen*. USPS initiated a comment period on April 2nd to begin revising its regulations accordingly. That is the kind of structural change that outlasts any single administration.

None of this collapses because Bondi is gone. Dhillon remains. The section remains. The cases remain. But who sits at the top of DOJ absolutely shapes how hard those cases get litigated, how much the solicitor general's office pushes, and whether the federal government files in support of gun owners or quietly lets them carry the load alone.

## The Zeldin question

Lee Zeldin is reportedly Trump's leading choice for the permanent Senate-confirmed role. His congressional record is genuinely solid. He earned an A rating from the NRA during his time in the House, co-sponsored national concealed carry reciprocity legislation, and during his 2022 New York gubernatorial run called for repealing the SAFE Act and celebrated *Bruen* when it landed. [Time's profile](https://time.com/article/2026/04/02/lee-zeldin-attorney-general-pam-bondi-trump-fire/) confirms he has been Trump's top rumored pick. [Bearing Arms noted](https://bearingarms.com/camedwards/2026/04/02/bondi-out-doj-civil-rights-division-2a-focus-n1232093/) that Zeldin could end up being an even stronger advocate for using DOJ against state gun control laws than Bondi was, given his legislative history.

My honest read: Zeldin is not a downgrade. His record is better than Bondi's. But the confirmation hearing will tell us a lot, and a congressman's voting record does not automatically translate into aggressive enforcement posture at a cabinet level. The history of this town is full of politicians who talked a good game on guns until they had power.

For now, Blanche is the one running things. And the infrastructure Dhillon has built does not go anywhere. The litigation continues. The Second Amendment Section keeps staffing up. The question is how hard DOJ pushes at the top when the political heat gets turned up, and on that front, Blanche has given us more reasons for confidence than Bondi ever did.

That is not nothing. Right now, it is actually quite a lot.
