## The Bottom Line

Pam Bondi's time as attorney general is over, and Todd Blanche is now running the Department of Justice. For Second Amendment supporters, this is about as good an outcome as was realistically on the table.

## What actually happened

On April 2, 2026, President Trump fired Attorney General Pam Bondi. Trump announced it matter-of-factly, praised her for her service in the administration, and named Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche as acting attorney general while the administration weighs a permanent replacement. EPA administrator Lee Zeldin has been floated as a candidate for the Senate-confirmed role.

The stated reasons center on frustration over the handling of the Jeffrey Epstein files and a perceived lack of aggressiveness in pursuing the administration's priorities. Whatever the internal politics, the practical result for the gun community is that Blanche is now running DOJ, and that matters.

## Bondi's record was never great on guns

I want to be fair here. Bondi's DOJ was not a disaster for gun owners. The people she installed beneath her, including Blanche himself and Harmeet Dhillon at the Civil Rights Division, were genuinely committed to Second Amendment enforcement. The department's posture under Bondi improved significantly over the Biden years. Credit where it's due.

But Bondi herself was never a principled Second Amendment defender, and the record in Florida makes that clear. As Florida's attorney general after the 2018 Parkland shooting, she praised the Marjory Stoneman Douglas High School Public Safety Act, a law that included a red flag provision, an effective ban on handgun purchases for anyone under 21, and a bump stock ban. She actively defended the 18-to-20 handgun purchase ban in court. Gun Owners of America flagged her mixed record publicly when she was nominated, and they were right to do so.

Her confirmation hearing did not help. When pressed on gun policy, she told senators she would "enforce the laws of the land" and signaled openness to reviewing red flag proposals at the federal level. That is not the posture you want from an attorney general who sits above ATF.

She was never the enemy. But there's a difference between "not the enemy" and "someone who actually believes this stuff."

## Why Blanche is different

Todd Blanche spent most of 2025 as the deputy attorney general, and he used that platform to show up for gun owners in ways that went well beyond symbolic gestures. He attended SHOT Show 2026 in Las Vegas, met with firearm industry members on the floor, sat in on the ATF town hall, and publicly told Breitbart that anti-gun advocates are "crafty" and "creative" in their ongoing violations of the Second Amendment. That is not the language of a politician hedging his bets. That is someone who actually understands what the gun rights movement has been up against.

At the SHOT Show ATF session, Blanche signaled concrete action on the rights restoration program, which processes applications from people who have had their federal firearms rights stripped and want them back. That program was essentially frozen under the Biden administration. Blanche indicated it would move. He also suggested the DOJ would take additional legal action against states that continue to restrict Second Amendment rights in defiance of **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**.

In my view, the distinction between Bondi and Blanche is not trivial. One was a competent administrator who kept the trains running and surrounded herself with good people on guns. The other is someone who genuinely believes the Second Amendment means what it says and is willing to use the full weight of the Department of Justice to say so.

## What this means in practice

The attorney general has direct authority over ATF and sets the enforcement priorities that determine which regulations get challenged, which get defended, and which get quietly shelved. That is a lot of power over a policy area where the last two administrations spent considerable energy making things worse.

The most important near-term question is what DOJ does with ongoing litigation. There are active cases right now over pistol braces, suppressor regulations, the bump stock ban's legal aftermath, and a range of state-level laws being challenged under Bruen. Who sits at the top of DOJ shapes how aggressively those cases get litigated, how much the solicitor general's office pushes, and whether the federal government files amicus briefs in support of gun owners or quietly lets challengers twist in the wind.

Under Blanche, I expect the department to be more willing to let the constitutionally questionable regulations die quietly rather than burning resources defending them in court. The gun community pushed hard for exactly that posture and never fully got it under Bondi.

## The Zeldin question

Lee Zeldin has an A rating from the NRA, which tells you something but not everything. His record in Congress on gun issues was generally solid. As EPA administrator he has had zero visibility on Second Amendment matters, which makes it hard to say much with confidence.

If Zeldin gets the nod for the permanent role, I think the realistic expectation is that DOJ's trajectory stays roughly where Blanche is pointing it, without the same personal commitment Blanche brings. That's not a disaster. But it is a step down from having someone in the chair who spent the last year actually working the issue.

The permanent replacement matters. The confirmation hearing will tell us a lot. For now, Blanche is running things, and for the time being, that's the best news Second Amendment supporters have had out of DOJ in a while.
