## The Bottom Line

Virginia's HB 702 mandates that every law enforcement agency in the state establish a gun buyback or give-back program by January 1, 2028. The research on buybacks is unambiguous: they don't reduce crime. This bill was never designed to reduce crime. It was designed to build the apparatus for something else entirely.

## What the bill actually does

Virginia's HB 702 passed both chambers of the General Assembly and was headed to Governor Abigail Spanberger's desk. The bill requires every local law enforcement agency in the state to develop policies and procedures for implementing a firearm give-back or buyback program. The deadline for compliance is January 1, 2028. This is not a voluntary option for localities that want to run these programs. It is a mandate. Every sheriff's department, every city police force, every law enforcement agency in Virginia must have this infrastructure in place.

The program's mechanics are worth understanding precisely. A person surrenders a firearm. Their identity is kept confidential, meaning the transaction is not recorded in any way that could be used against them. The firearm is then subjected to forensic testing. If it isn't connected to a crime, it is destroyed within 90 days, with a narrow exception for firearms of historical or collector value manufactured before 1945, which may be auctioned to a licensed FFL dealer. Everything else is gone.

On the surface this sounds like a typical buyback program. Localities run these events periodically, people bring in guns they don't want, the guns are destroyed. It sounds inoffensive enough that the politics of opposing it are uncomfortable. Who is against getting unwanted guns off the street?

The answer to that question requires looking at what the research actually shows about these programs, and then asking a harder question about what this bill is actually for.

## What we know about buybacks

RAND Corporation researchers, who are not a pro-gun organization, examined the evidence on gun buyback programs and found little support for the claim that they reduce firearm-related crime or death. Multiple peer-reviewed studies reach the same conclusion. Dr. Will Pelfrey of Virginia Commonwealth University's Wilder School of Government and Public Affairs has said directly that "gun buyback programs do not have a measurable impact on crime rates, particularly violent crime rates."

The reasons are straightforward. People who commit crimes with guns do not voluntarily surrender them to police. The guns that get turned in at buybacks are overwhelmingly owned by older, non-violent people who have a firearm they no longer want. Those guns were not going to be used in crimes. Their removal from circulation has no effect on the people or the firearms that actually drive violence statistics.

There is also a well-documented perverse incentive problem. When buybacks offer cash or gift cards, people build or acquire cheap firearms specifically to cash them out. During various buyback events around the country, participants have turned in 3D-printed guns and homemade pipe guns costing a few dollars in exchange for gift cards worth fifty dollars or more. The programs functionally subsidize the production of low-quality firearms that are then handed directly to the government.

None of this is disputed in the academic literature. The people who designed HB 702 know this. They had access to the same research. The bill was not written in ignorance of the evidence. It was written despite it.

## The ghost gun connection

To understand what HB 702 is actually doing, you have to look at it alongside HB 40, which passed the Virginia legislature in the same session. HB 40 criminalizes the manufacture, possession, or transfer of unserialized firearms, making it a Class 5 felony. So-called ghost guns, home-built firearms, and 3D-printed firearms without serial numbers are banned outright.

The logic connecting these two bills is not subtle. If every legally purchased firearm in Virginia has a traceable serial number registered to an identifiable buyer at an FFL, and manufacturing an unserialized firearm is a felony, the state approaches a functional inventory of every legally owned gun in the commonwealth. The buyback infrastructure then exists as a mechanism for collection.

I want to be careful here because I don't think HB 702 by itself constitutes confiscation. The bill is explicit that participation is voluntary. No one is being compelled to surrender a firearm under this law. What it does is establish, in every jurisdiction in Virginia, the operational capacity to run a collection program. The personnel know the procedures. The paperwork exists. The destruction timeline is codified. The only thing missing is the mandate to participate.

That mandate could come later. Advocates who dismiss this concern as paranoid are ignoring the explicit logic of the legislative sequence. You don't ban unserialized firearms and then simultaneously mandate buyback infrastructure in every locality because you're interested in voluntary community engagement. You do it because you're building toward something.

## The reciprocity issue

There is a specific flaw in HB 702's design that reveals something important about its priorities. The bill requires that the identity of anyone surrendering a firearm be kept confidential and that the transaction not be recorded in a way admissible in court.

The stated rationale is that this encourages people to turn in firearms without fear of prosecution. But the practical effect is that someone who steals a firearm can surrender it at a buyback, collect whatever compensation is offered, and face no legal consequences because the transaction is confidential and non-admissible. The state has created a laundering mechanism for stolen property. This is not an oversight. It is a design choice, and it suggests that the bill's actual priorities are about maximizing the number of guns collected, not about public safety or property rights.

## Mandatory participation, not optional

The most significant feature of HB 702 is the one that gets the least attention: there is no opt-out for localities. Virginia operates under the Dillon Rule, which means local governments have only the powers expressly granted by the state. In this case, the state is not just granting permission to run buyback programs. It is requiring them. A conservative sheriff in a rural Virginia county who has no interest in running these programs and whose constituents overwhelmingly oppose them has no legal recourse. The mandate is statewide and non-negotiable.

This is consistent with how the broader package of Virginia gun legislation is structured. Preemption law in Virginia generally prevents localities from enacting gun restrictions more stringent than state law. But when the state itself mandates a program, that preemption structure doesn't help. The Second Amendment sanctuary resolutions passed by more than 90 Virginia counties and cities in 2019 and after carry no legal weight here. A resolution affirming Second Amendment rights does not exempt a sheriff's department from complying with a state mandate to establish buyback infrastructure.

## What "voluntary" means right now

In my view, the word "voluntary" in this bill is doing a lot of heavy lifting that it will not always be able to sustain. Right now, in 2026, participation for gun owners is voluntary. You can ignore these programs completely. But the infrastructure will exist in every jurisdiction, fully operational, by 2028. Whether it stays voluntary depends entirely on what the Virginia legislature does in future sessions.

What Virginia has passed this session tells you something about those future sessions. An assault weapons ban. A ghost gun prohibition that criminalizes the manufacture of unserialized firearms. Red flag procedures that allow seizures without criminal conviction. A restriction on concealed carry reciprocity. A law funding anti-gun advocacy organizations with state money. A working group charged with producing the next round of legislation. And now a mandate that every locality in the state have a firearm collection and destruction program fully operational within two years.

This is a policy architecture, not a collection of unrelated bills. Each piece supports the others. The buyback infrastructure is the final piece of a system that tracks, restricts, and ultimately aims to reduce the number of legally owned firearms in Virginia through any means available.

I think gun owners should take the word "voluntary" seriously for exactly as long as this legislature keeps it in the bill.
