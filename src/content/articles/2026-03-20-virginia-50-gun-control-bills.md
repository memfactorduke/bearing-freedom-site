## The Bottom Line

Virginia's 2026 legislative session produced more than 50 gun-related bills in a single session, and the volume is the strategy. When defenders of the Second Amendment have to track an AR-15 ban, a statewide public carry prohibition, a red flag expansion, a ghost gun ban, a reciprocity rollback, and a mandatory buyback infrastructure all at once, things slip through. That's the point.

## Flood the zone

Virginia's 2026 regular session has been, by any honest measure, the most aggressive legislative attack on gun rights in the Commonwealth's history. Democrats in Richmond sent more than 18 gun control bills to Governor Abigail Spanberger's desk, with the governor having until April 13th to act. They tried to pass more than 50 in total. The sheer quantity is not accidental.

This is a deliberate tactic. Pass enough bills simultaneously and even the most engaged Second Amendment advocates can't give adequate attention to all of them. The headline grabbers — an AR-15 ban, a magazine cap — consume the oxygen while the structural legislation quietly clears both chambers. By the time anyone maps the full picture, it's too late to fight individual bills. In my view, the only appropriate response is to document every single piece of this and see the package for what it is: a coordinated disarmament strategy pursued through legislative attrition.

## The ban on rifles most Virginians legally own

**SB 749** and its House companion **HB 217** are the bills that made national news, and they deserve the attention. The legislation bans the sale, manufacture, import, purchase, and transfer of so-called "assault firearms" and limits magazines to 15 rounds. Existing owners get to keep what they have, but they cannot transfer those firearms to anyone else. When they die, the firearm effectively dies with them.

That grandfathering scheme is, in some ways, worse than a straight confiscation. At least a confiscation order is legally challengeable on its face. What Virginia has instead is a slow phase-out — an arrangement where the right is not technically taken from you, but it withers away with each passing generation. The [Reload reported](https://thereload.com/virginia-legislature-passes-ar-15-ammo-mag-sales-ban/) that SB 749 passed the Senate 21-19, a narrow margin that reflects how polarized the state has become on this issue, and how close this would have been with a single seat flipping.

**SB 727** received far less coverage, and I think it's arguably the more immediately dangerous bill. It prohibits carrying defined "assault firearms" on any public street, road, alley, sidewalk, public right-of-way, or any open public space in the Commonwealth. This is not just about AR-15s. The bill's definition of "assault firearm" sweeps in semi-automatic pistols that accept detachable magazines over certain capacities. A standard-capacity Glock carried lawfully under Virginia's existing concealed carry law could qualify as an "assault firearm" under SB 727's definitions. The penalty is a Class 1 misdemeanor — a criminal charge for carrying what has been, until this session, a perfectly lawful firearm.

## Banning the guns the government doesn't know about

**HB 40** and its Senate companion **SB 323** criminalize the manufacture, possession, and transfer of unserialized firearms. A first offense for possession is a misdemeanor. A second is a felony-class offense. The stated rationale is preventing "ghost guns" from bypassing background check systems.

I want to explain why I think this framing misses the deeper issue. Home manufacture of firearms is among the oldest and most well-established traditions of American gun ownership. At the founding, the overwhelming majority of civilian firearms were made locally, not by large manufacturers. The right to keep and bear arms was built around the idea that you could obtain and possess a firearm independent of institutional channels. Virginia's HB 40 effectively requires that every firearm in the Commonwealth have a government-tracked serial number — meaning the government, in some form, knows about every gun you own. That concern becomes more acute when you note that the same legislative package includes mandatory buyback infrastructure at every law enforcement agency in the state.

## Red flags, expanded and normalized

**House Bill 901** broadens the pool of people who can petition a court for an emergency risk protective order — Virginia's version of a red flag law. Under the existing framework, courts can already remove firearms from someone before any criminal conviction or finding of guilt. The expansion simply means more people can initiate that process against you.

The constitutional problem with red flag laws is not complicated. Due process requires that the government establish you're dangerous before taking your property and restricting your rights, not after. Virginia's ERPO scheme inverts that sequence. You lose your guns first, and the hearing comes later. **HB 1071** compounds this by mandating annual training on emergency risk orders for staff at public schools and higher education institutions — not to prevent abuse of the process, but to make the process more routinely used. Training more people to file ERPO petitions means more petitions get filed, more of which will be erroneous or retaliatory.

## The infrastructure for confiscation

**House Bill 702** requires every law enforcement agency in Virginia to establish a firearm give-back or buyback program. On its face, this sounds like a feel-good community policing measure. I think it's something more deliberate.

A mandatory buyback infrastructure at every law enforcement agency in the state is exactly what you would build in step one if your long-term goal was a mandatory surrender program. The legal and logistical framework to accept large volumes of firearms already exists once you pass HB 702. The machinery is in place. All that's required to flip voluntary to mandatory is a future bill. Gun Owners of America has [pledged to challenge](https://www.gunowners.org/va03052026/) several of these measures in court, including the assault weapons ban, but litigation takes years. Infrastructure, once built, tends to stay.

## Catching you out

**SB 115** restructures how Virginia handles concealed carry reciprocity with other states. Rather than automatic recognition of out-of-state permits, the bill gives the superintendent of state police discretion to determine whether another state's qualifications are "substantially similar" to Virginia's. The attorney general's office must review all existing reciprocity agreements by December 2026 and revoke any that don't meet the new standard by July 2027.

If you live in a neighboring state, hold a valid permit, and regularly cross into Virginia, you may find yourself suddenly disarmed — not because of anything you did, but because a bureaucrat in Richmond decided your home state's training requirements aren't close enough to Virginia's. The bill also voids out-of-state permits for Virginia residents immediately, meaning anyone who recently moved to the state has no valid permit until they complete Virginia's own process.

Two other bills deserve mention for their design as legal traps. **HB 9009** creates a firearms exclusion zone around polling places, including absentee drop-off locations. Most permit holders have no practical way to know where every absentee location is on any given day. Drive past one while lawfully carrying and you're a criminal. **Senate Bill 348**'s safe storage requirements similarly turn normal home defense practices into potential misdemeanors — a firearm in a nightstand is not "locked up" within the bill's definition, which raises the question of how a legally armed Virginian is supposed to actually defend themselves at 3 a.m.

## What this package actually is

Virginia's Second Amendment community includes roughly 90 Second Amendment sanctuary localities — counties and cities where local sheriffs and governing bodies have pledged not to enforce certain gun control measures. That matters as a cultural signal, but it's not a reliable shield. Sanctuary resolutions don't void state law. If the political winds shift or a new sheriff takes office, every one of those residents is potentially exposed to laws they thought they were safe from.

The full picture of this session is two tracks operating simultaneously. One track is long-term disarmament infrastructure: mandatory buyback programs, ghost gun bans, serialization requirements, all building a system in which the government knows where the guns are and has the machinery to take them back when it chooses. The other track is immediate attrition: every possible legal mechanism to reduce the number of Virginians who are armed, carry in public, or can afford to stay in the market.

In my view, this is the most dangerous kind of gun control precisely because it's diffuse. There's no single bill to rally against, no one AR-15 ban to litigate to the Supreme Court. There are fifty bills, most of which individually look unremarkable, and which collectively amount to a comprehensive disarmament program. Virginia residents have until April 13th to make their voices heard to Governor Spanberger. After that, the courts will be the last line of defense — and the courts move slowly.
