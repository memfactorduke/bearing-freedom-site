## The Bottom Line

Virginia's HB9009 isn't a polling place bill. It's a year-round, city-wide carry ban wearing a polling place costume. Your handgun permit is now worthless in eleven different location categories you can't find on any map, and there's no required signage to warn you before you walk into a criminal charge.

## Polling places, they said

When Virginia legislators introduced HB9009, they framed it as a modest update to existing law. The old statute banned firearms within 40 feet of a polling place entrance while polls were open. Reasonable people can debate that. The Supreme Court in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** listed polling places among historically recognized sensitive locations, and there is at least a colorable argument for that limited restriction.

HB9009 is not that restricted. The bill expands the exclusion radius from 40 to 100 feet, which sounds like a number that changed. In reality, because area scales with the square of the radius, that is a roughly 525% increase in the restricted zone around every building it touches. Every ballot drop box, every satellite registration site, every room where electoral board members vote to certify results now carries a bubble the size of a small city block, and you are a criminal the moment you cross the invisible perimeter.

## Eleven categories, none of them clearly marked

The real story is not the radius. It's the list. Virginia's prior law covered what most people think of as polling places: the building where you vote on Election Day. HB9009 covers eleven distinct location types, and the overwhelming majority of them operate year-round with no obvious public-facing presence.

Those locations include the principal office of the general registrar, satellite voter registration sites, central absentee precincts where absentee ballots are processed, any building where the local electoral board meets, any building used by the State Board of Elections to canvass results, recount locations, provisional ballot locations, and ballot drop boxes. Many of these are tucked inside government office buildings. Some are in strip malls. Several rotate. You may work in the same building as one without knowing it.

None of these locations are required to post signage under the bill. The only location with a signage requirement is the actual polling place. Every other category is a legal minefield with no warning markers.

I think about what this looks like in practice. You drive to get coffee during an election season, passing a ballot drop box on a public sidewalk. You have a lawfully carried handgun. You may have just committed a Class 1 misdemeanor. The penalty structure is not light: up to 12 months in jail, a $2,500 fine, and a permanent criminal record.

## The handgun permit exemption is gone

Under previous Virginia law, concealed handgun permit holders had an exemption from some of the polling place firearms restrictions. That exemption is repealed entirely by HB9009. The logic of a carry permit is that the state has vetted you, found no disqualifying factors, and extended a degree of trust. This bill says that vetting is meaningless within 100 feet of any of eleven location categories you cannot reliably identify in advance.

There is also no exemption for active duty or reserve military members. The people the government trusts to carry weapons overseas have no carve-out here. The only people who walk through these zones without criminal exposure are licensed armed security covered by the Law Enforcement Officers Safety Act and sworn law enforcement. Everyone else, regardless of permit status or military service, is subject to arrest.

## "Carry on or about his person"

One change in the bill's language that hasn't gotten enough attention is the shift from "possesses" to "carries on or about his person." That distinction matters because it raises questions about what happens when you are in a vehicle. If you are lawfully transporting a firearm in your car and you drive within 100 feet of a ballot drop box, you may be within the bill's reach. The law is not written with a transport carve-out for these new categories the way Virginia's general transport statutes are.

This creates a scenario that would strike most reasonable people as absurd but is legally coherent under the bill's text: a driver involved in a rear-end collision, carrying legally under a Virginia permit, is found by responding officers to be within 82 feet of a ballot drop box they didn't know existed. The permit is no defense. The fact that the location is unmarked is no defense. The statute does not include a knowledge requirement.

## Bruen doesn't reach this far

The Supreme Court was clear in **Bruen** that sensitive place designations cannot be used to swallow the right. The Court rejected New York's attempt to designate all crowded areas as sensitive, noting there is no historical tradition of effectively banning carry in all public spaces by accumulating location exemptions. Virginia is attempting something similar: using the peripheral legitimacy of polling place restrictions to create a category of year-round, unmarked, roving gun-free zones covering an enormous share of urban geography.

The historical analogs the Court cited in **Bruen** for polling places were narrow. The 1742 Virginia example that scholars cite involved courthouse voting with guards posted at the door, a far cry from a 100-foot radius around a registration satellite office inside an office park that operates every business day of the year. In my view, most of HB9009's eleven location categories have no plausible historical analog whatsoever, and the year-round nature of the restrictions for non-polling-place sites further distances the law from any historical tradition.

## The design of ignorance as a trap

What makes this bill genuinely dangerous, separate from its constitutional problems, is that it is structured so that ordinary people cannot avoid violating it. A rational gun owner who wants to comply with the law cannot simply check a map, because Virginia does not maintain a publicly accessible database of every satellite registration site, every electoral board meeting room, or every absentee processing location. These move. They change. Absentee counting may happen in a church one cycle and a government building the next.

The bill's authors made no effort to require the state to publish and maintain a searchable registry of restricted locations. They made no effort to require signage. They stripped the handgun permit exemption that would have protected the most careful, law-abiding gun owners. Every one of those choices points toward a law designed not to be followed but to be triggered selectively against people who have no way to know they are in violation.

That is not public safety legislation. In my view, it is a mechanism for turning lawful gun owners into criminals without their knowledge, and the people who wrote this bill knew exactly what they were building.
