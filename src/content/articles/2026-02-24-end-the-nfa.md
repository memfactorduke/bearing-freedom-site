## The Bottom Line

West Virginia's Senate Bill 1071 proposed using a carveout written into the Hughes Amendment itself to let the state sell machine guns directly to qualified residents, legally bypassing the 1986 federal ban. The Senate President killed it before it could pass, citing drafting problems. But the legal theory behind it is sound, the crack it found in federal law is real, and that idea is not going back in the box.

## What the Hughes Amendment actually says

Most people who know anything about the NFA understand the basics of the 1986 machine gun ban: the Firearm Owners' Protection Act included a last-minute amendment offered by Representative William Hughes of New Jersey that froze the civilian machine gun registry at the date of enactment, May 19, 1986. Any machine gun manufactured and registered before that date can still be transferred between civilians. Anything manufactured after that date cannot. That's the provision that turned a transferable pre-1986 M16 into a $30,000 collector's item while the functionally identical rifle in your local law enforcement armory can be purchased new for a few hundred dollars.

What gets discussed far less often is the exemption language built directly into 18 U.S.C. § 922(o). The subsection that bans civilian machine gun possession explicitly does not apply to "a transfer to or by, or possession by or under the authority of, the United States or any department or agency thereof or a State, or a department, agency, or political subdivision thereof." A state, in other words, is categorically exempted from the machine gun transfer prohibition. That's not an interpretation or a creative reading. It's the plain text.

West Virginia Senate Bill 1071, introduced by Senators Chris Rose and Zack Maynard, proposed creating the Office of Public Defense within the West Virginia State Police. That office would procure machine guns, including post-1986 machine guns not available to civilians under current law, and transfer them to qualified residents who passed a background check. Because the state itself would be making the transfer, and because the state is explicitly exempted from the transfer prohibition, the federal ban arguably would not apply. Residents would pay a processing fee and receive something that has been functionally off-limits to civilians for forty years.

## Why this was more significant than it looked

I'll be honest: I find it embarrassing that this legal theory sat in plain sight for four decades without anyone running a serious legislative test of it. The NFA has always been the weakest link in the legal architecture of gun control, but it has also attracted the least sustained legal attention. The major Second Amendment organizations have focused on carry rights, assault weapons bans, handgun rosters, red flag laws, and age restrictions. All of those fights are important. None of them touch the NFA.

The reason is partly cultural. Most gun owners, even committed Second Amendment advocates, have internalized the post-1986 machine gun ban as a fixed reality. The registry is closed. Pre-86 transferables are prohibitively expensive. Full-auto ownership is something that existed in a different era. That fatalism has real consequences: it means there has been no sustained pressure on Congress or in the courts to revisit the constitutionality of the Hughes Amendment, even as **Bruen** and **Heller** fundamentally changed the analytical framework courts are supposed to apply to firearms restrictions.

The Fifth Circuit upheld 922(o) as recently as January 2026. A federal district court in Mississippi ruled the opposite way. The circuit split is forming. What has been missing is the legislative pressure from states that would force federal courts to take the question seriously. West Virginia's bill was exactly that kind of pressure.

## The NFA's internal logic has always been absurd

It's worth stepping back from the specific legal maneuver to say something plainly: the National Firearms Act and the Hughes Amendment are incoherent law.

A pistol is legal. Add a piece of plastic to the grip and attach a short barrel, and you have a short-barreled rifle, which requires an NFA tax stamp and a six-to-twelve-month ATF wait. Make the barrel a bit longer and it's legal again. A standard rifle barrel is fine. Cut it two inches short of the legal minimum and you're facing ten years in federal prison. Suppressors, which are standard safety equipment throughout much of Europe and required for civilian use in several countries to protect hearing, are regulated as if they're assassination tools because legislators in 1934 worried that police wouldn't be able to hear shots fired. Someone literally registered a potato as a suppressor earlier this year to demonstrate how arbitrary the regulatory category is.

Machine guns, as a category, have been effectively unavailable to ordinary civilians since 1986 despite the fact that their ownership was entirely legal for the previous fifty-two years of American history. The constitutional question is whether the government can simply close a registry and eliminate a previously lawful class of ownership going forward, particularly under a post-**Bruen** framework that requires regulations to be rooted in the historical tradition of firearms regulation. Nothing in American firearms history prior to 1986 supports a categorical ban on civilian machine gun ownership.

## What actually happened to SB 1071

Senate President Randy Smith blocked the bill before it could reach a floor vote, saying it was "poorly drafted" and that counsel had advised him there was no existing case law permitting a state to transfer machine guns to private citizens in the way the bill envisioned. Smith said he consulted with NRA representatives and the West Virginia Citizens Defense League, both of whom reportedly agreed the bill would face significant legal challenges.

That's a legitimate concern about execution. A bill that invites immediate federal injunction and fails in court does more damage to the underlying legal theory than no bill at all. But there's a difference between "this specific bill needs better draftsmanship" and "this approach can't work." The exemption in 922(o) says what it says. The question is whether a state can structure a transfer program that falls within that exemption in a way that survives judicial scrutiny, and the honest answer is that no court has definitively resolved that question because no state has tried in a sustained way.

Gun Owners of America has been pushing back on Smith's decision. Senator Rose, the bill's sponsor, has been publicly critical of the process by which it was killed. The West Virginia Citizens Defense League's position, according to Smith, was that the bill needed more work before moving forward, which is different from saying the theory behind it is wrong.

## The broader template

What makes this bill genuinely important beyond West Virginia is that it demonstrates a class of legislative strategy that pro-Second Amendment states have barely begun to explore. The Hughes Amendment's state exemption is one example. The concealed carry reciprocity question has similar potential: a state that structures a program allowing residents to carry in an official capacity as part of a community defense program could potentially give those residents carry rights that track them across state lines under federal law governing law enforcement officers. These aren't tricks or loopholes in a pejorative sense. They're legitimate uses of statutory language as written.

The gun control movement has spent decades finding creative legislative strategies to regulate firearms at the margins of what the Constitution permits. In my view, it's past time for pro-rights states to apply the same creativity in the other direction, reading federal statutes carefully to find every available avenue for restoring rights that have been incrementally stripped away.

West Virginia didn't get this one over the finish line in the 2026 session. But the idea is now in circulation. Other states are watching. The next version will be drafted more carefully, and at some point, one of them is going to pass.
