## The arithmetic of overwhelm

By the time Virginia's 2026 General Assembly session ended on March 14, Democrats had pushed approximately 25 gun reform bills across the finish line and sent them to Governor Abigail Spanberger's desk. The signing deadline was April 13th. That is not a legislative session. That is an occupation.

I want to be specific about what this means in practice. These were not 25 bills introduced over years of deliberate debate. They arrived in a compressed window, many of them companion bills running identical provisions through both chambers simultaneously, designed to create redundancy and confusion in equal measure. Advocates, journalists, and ordinary gun owners were forced to track more information than any reasonable person can absorb. That capacity problem is not an unfortunate side effect of the process. It is the process.

The strategy has a name. Political operatives use the phrase "flood the zone." You move so many things simultaneously that the opposition's attention is fragmented, media coverage is diluted across dozens of items, and the big-ticket wins sail through on the tailwind of general confusion. Virginia Democrats executed this with precision. The AR-15 ban, HB 217 and its Senate companion SB 749, captured almost all available media oxygen while 29 other measures moved through with minimal scrutiny.

## What actually passed

It's worth looking at several of these bills in detail because the full picture is more alarming than the headline ban.

HB 40 makes it a Class 5 felony to manufacture, import, sell, transfer, or possess an unserialized firearm. That includes 3D-printed firearms and parts kits. This bill criminalizes something that was entirely lawful for most of American history: building your own gun. The craftsmanship tradition is constitutionally significant, and the Supreme Court's 2022 ruling in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** requires that modern gun laws find an analog in the nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation. There is no historical analog for criminalizing home gunsmithing. The bill's real function is registration by elimination: if every legally purchased firearm has a traceable serial number and home manufacture is a felony, the government approaches a complete inventory of every gun in the state.

SB 115 effectively destroys concealed carry reciprocity with other states. It gives the state police superintendent discretionary authority to decide whether another state's permitting system is "substantially similar" to Virginia's. In practice, this means that a North Carolina resident with a valid carry permit who crosses into Virginia for work is now at significant legal risk. That includes members of the military stationed near state borders and countless commuters who have been lawfully carrying for years. The bill is not written to protect anyone from violence. It is written to reduce the number of people carrying guns.

HB 901 and its Senate companion SB 495 establish red flag procedures allowing courts to order firearm seizures without a criminal conviction, and in many cases without the gun owner even being present to contest the order. The due process problems here are substantial. These orders can be based on hearsay, issued ex parte, and the person subject to the order has no opportunity to challenge the evidence before their property is seized. The point, as I see it, is to make the process the punishment. Being innocent does not protect you from the costs and disruptions of having your guns taken.

HB 348 and its Senate companion impose safe storage requirements in any home where a minor or prohibited person is present. I understand the impulse behind this kind of law. But the way these statutes are typically written, a firearm secured in a nightstand drawer does not meet the standard because it isn't under the owner's "immediate control." If you are sleeping and someone breaks into your house, you are expected to reach the gun safe, enter a combination, locate your ammunition separately, and assemble a response. No serious person believes this is how defensive gun use works in practice. The bill makes fathers less able to defend their children, and that is not a secondary effect. It's predictable.

SB 364 is the one that should concern gun owners who are thinking past this session. It establishes state funding for "gun violence prevention" organizations and programs. Your tax money will flow to advocacy groups whose explicit mission is generating more gun control legislation. Virginia's Department of Criminal Justice Services already administers a related grant program. SB 364 formalizes and expands that pipeline. The infrastructure of perpetual restriction is being built at public expense.

## The constitutional reckoning ahead

Gun Owners of America has announced it will challenge the assault weapons ban in court. House Minority Leader Terry Kilgore described HB 217 as a law that "blatantly defies" the **Bruen** standard, and he's right to raise it. **Bruen** requires the government to demonstrate that a gun restriction is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation. A ban on semi-automatic rifles and pistols that are owned by tens of millions of Americans and used overwhelmingly for lawful purposes cannot find that historical analog. No one was banning commonly owned firearms in 1791.

The 4th Circuit, which covers Virginia, has historically been hostile to Second Amendment claims. But the Supreme Court's language in **Bruen** was not ambiguous, and lower courts that have tried to work around it have seen their reasoning questioned. The Virginia assault weapons ban is going to produce litigation. Whether that litigation succeeds depends on how willing the federal judiciary is to follow **Bruen** to its logical conclusions.

## What the sanctuary movement tells us

In 2019, when Democrats first took control of the Virginia legislature after years of Republican dominance, more than 90 Virginia counties and cities passed Second Amendment sanctuary resolutions. That movement was partly symbolic but not entirely. It reflected genuine, widespread resistance among Virginia's rural and suburban gun-owning communities to what they correctly anticipated was coming.

What came in 2020 was significant but restrained compared to what arrived in 2026. The difference is that Spanberger's administration entered this session with a fully developed legislative agenda, experienced staff who had been planning this for years, and a national support infrastructure from organizations like Everytown and Moms Demand Action that provided both template legislation and political cover.

In my view, the sanctuary resolutions were the right instinct expressed in the wrong form. Resolutions don't block legislation. Organized political opposition does. The Virginia Citizens Defense League tracked every bill this session and mobilized testimony against dozens of them. Roughly 20 of the approximately 58 gun-related bills introduced were killed. That is a real result. It matters. But it is not sufficient when the other side is playing a long game with institutional resources and a clear multi-session strategy.

## The machine that keeps producing

What concerns me more than any individual bill is the structure being put in place to keep producing them. HB 969 directs the Department of Criminal Justice Services to form a working group of "firearm safety advocates" to generate policy recommendations. That working group's output becomes next session's bill package. The machine is self-replicating.

SB 364's funding mechanism means that organizations advocating for more restrictions will have access to state resources to build their capacity, run public communications campaigns, and develop legislative proposals. Gun rights organizations in Virginia operate on member dues and donations. The other side will soon have a taxpayer subsidy.

This is where I think the fight in Virginia actually is. Not just at the bill level, where advocates are already doing the work, but at the institutional level. Every grant the Department of Criminal Justice Services awards to a gun control organization, every working group convened under HB 969, every line in the budget directed toward "firearm violence prevention" represents a durable shift in the state's capacity to keep moving in this direction regardless of which party controls the legislature in future sessions.

Virginia gun owners won some battles this session. Twenty bills were stopped. That matters. But the session's real legacy may be the permanent infrastructure it created for the next one.
