## The Bottom Line

Virginia's HB702 doesn't confiscate a single gun today, and that is precisely the point. By 2028, every county and city law enforcement agency in the state will be required to have a functioning firearm collection and destruction apparatus. The infrastructure will be there, waiting, when the next law tells it what to do.

## What the bill actually does

Virginia HB702 establishes the Virginia Firearm Giveback Program and Fund, mandating that every county and city law enforcement agency in the Commonwealth develop and implement either a firearm give-back or buyback program by January 1, 2028. Town agencies may participate voluntarily. County and city agencies have no choice.

The bill passed along entirely party-line votes and now sits on Governor Abigail Spanberger's desk with an action deadline of April 13, 2026. There is no realistic expectation she will veto it. She does not need to sign it to let it become law.

Firearms at least 40 years old may be exempt from mandatory destruction depending on how certain conference provisions are applied, and the law was modified in conference to allow localities to retain and sell collected firearms rather than destroy them automatically. Those are minor softening amendments around what remains a statewide mandate to build confiscation-ready infrastructure in every locality in Virginia.

## The "giveback" framing is not an accident

The bill is officially called a giveback program. I find that worth dwelling on, because the name does a specific kind of political work.

Calling it a "giveback" implies the government gave you something you are now returning. The government did not give you your firearm. You bought it, inherited it, or manufactured it. The right to own it exists independent of state permission. Calling this a giveback frames the state as the original grantor of firearms rights, which it is not. Rights are not privileges extended by government that can be recalled when convenient.

"Buyback" has the same problem. You cannot buy back something you never sold. These are not semantic complaints. The language is designed to make property confiscation sound like civic participation.

## Three decades of research say it doesn't work

Gun buyback programs have been studied extensively in the United States and the evidence against their effectiveness is about as strong as social science evidence gets. The National Bureau of Economic Research examined buyback programs across 245 jurisdictions and found, at 95% confidence, they could rule out even a 1.1% reduction in firearm-related crime. A separate analysis found no evidence programs reduce suicides or homicides involving firearms.

The reason is structural. Voluntary buybacks collect guns from people who are not going to commit crimes. Someone planning to shoot another person is not dropping their firearm off at a police station for a gift card. The guns that show up at these events tend to be old, inoperable, or owned by people who wanted an easy way to dispose of an inherited firearm they never wanted in the first place. This is not a disputed political point, it is a consistent empirical finding replicated across decades of research.

RAND's analysis of gun policy evidence is similarly skeptical, noting that studies of U.S. buyback programs find little to no effect on shootings, homicides, or suicides over either short or long timeframes.

## The stolen gun problem

HB702 has a provision requiring that the identity of anyone who surrenders a firearm be kept confidential. This is framed as protecting participants' privacy. In practice, it creates a risk-free pathway to fence stolen property through a government program.

If I steal your firearm, I normally face significant risk trying to sell it. Pawn shops are regulated and keep records. Private sales leave traces. But under HB702, I can walk into the local law enforcement buyback event, turn in a stolen gun I did not own, collect a gift card with no questions asked, and have my identity shielded from any follow-up. The victim's property is destroyed. The thief is paid for stealing it. The state provided the mechanism.

People have already gamed less formal versions of this. In a 2008 Oakland buyback, local gun dealers bought cheap firearms out of state and sold them to the government for a profit. The "no questions asked" design is not a bug in buyback programs, it is a feature advocates insist on. In Virginia's mandatory program, that feature now applies across every county and city in the state.

## Why this particular bill is dangerous long term

The voluntary nature of individual participation is the part proponents point to when critics raise confiscation concerns. Nobody is forced to turn in a gun today. That is true. It is also beside the point.

What HB702 builds by 2028 is not just a series of collection events. It is a statewide administrative apparatus: collection procedures in every locality, annual reporting to State Police, destruction or disposition protocols, funding mechanisms. None of that existed before. All of it can be repurposed.

Consider what changes if a future Virginia legislature passes a mandatory surrender order for a category of firearms. Without HB702, implementing that order would require building collection infrastructure from scratch in 133 counties and independent cities simultaneously. With HB702, that infrastructure already exists in each one. The friction of implementation, which in a functioning constitutional republic serves a protective function, disappears.

I think the Supreme Court's current composition is likely to block mandatory confiscation under existing **Bruen** doctrine. That is a reasonable confidence, not a certainty. Courts change. Majorities change. Legal doctrine evolves. Building the administrative machinery now, under the cover of a voluntary program with sympathetic framing, is what political strategists call "creating optionality." It lowers the cost of a more aggressive policy if the legal or political environment ever permits it.

## Part of a much larger package

HB702 did not arrive alone. The 2026 Virginia legislative session produced more than 20 gun control bills, including HB217, an assault weapons ban that covers semi-automatic center-fire rifles and pistols with magazines over 20 rounds and is now awaiting Spanberger's signature. The volume was clearly calculated to overwhelm tracking capacity. Gun rights organizations, legal watchdogs, and media coverage were all spread thin across the full package. HB702 received a fraction of the attention given to the assault weapons bill.

That asymmetry in coverage was the strategy. The assault weapons ban is the fight everyone can see. The buyback infrastructure bill is the foundation being poured while everyone is watching the other fight.

In my view, Virginia gun owners and Second Amendment advocates around the country should pay considerably more attention to infrastructure legislation than they typically do. Headline bans generate lawsuits and political energy. Administrative machinery accumulates quietly. By the time it matters, it is already built.
