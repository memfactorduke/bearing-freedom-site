## The Bottom Line

Virginia's HB40 has no grandfather clause. Someone who legally built a firearm as a personal project before this law existed will wake up one day as a felon, owing the state nothing but compliance or prison. That is not gun safety legislation. That is a trap.

## What the law actually does

Virginia's HB40, passed in the 2026 legislative session alongside a wave of other gun control measures, prohibits the manufacture, possession, and transfer of unserialized firearms, unfinished frames and receivers, and certain plastic or 3D-printed firearms that can't clear a metal detector. Those three categories sound precise and narrow. In practice they cover a huge swath of entirely ordinary gun-owner behavior.

An 80% lower receiver, the raw aluminum block that hobbyists machine into an AR lower, is captured by this law as an "unfinished frame or receiver that can be readily completed." A home-built pistol assembled from legally purchased parts is captured as an "unserialized firearm." A polymer-framed 3D-printed pistol is potentially captured under the third category. None of these things require any criminal intent. None of them required any special licensing to own or build before this law. And critically, there is [no grandfather clause protecting anything you already have](https://vaghostguns.com/).

Possession becomes a crime on July 1, 2027. First offense: a Class 1 misdemeanor, up to 12 months in jail and a $2,500 fine. Second offense: a Class 4 felony, two to ten years in prison, and permanent, lifetime loss of all firearm rights. That second offense is the same felony classification as involuntary manslaughter in Virginia.

## The compliance path is a fiction

Virginia's legislature was apparently self-aware enough to know that retroactive criminalization of items people purchased and built legally is hard to justify publicly. So the bill includes a nominal path to compliance: take your unserialized firearm to a licensed dealer, have them imprint a serial number, problem solved.

Except that is not how gun shops work. [FFLs typically receive and transfer serialized firearms from manufacturers](https://vaghostguns.com/). They are not equipped to engrave serial numbers onto customer-owned receivers. The equipment, the liability, and the ATF recordkeeping requirements involved are not part of ordinary gunsmith or dealer operations. So in practice, the "just go get it serialized" option is unavailable to most Virginians who own these firearms.

What that leaves you with is a choice between turning your legally purchased property over to the government for nothing, or risking a felony conviction. That is the choice Virginia has decided to offer its own law-abiding residents.

## The historical argument is devastating

The Supreme Court's 2022 decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** established that firearms regulations must be rooted in America's historical tradition of firearm regulation at the founding. Under **Bruen**, the government bears the burden of identifying a historical analogue for any modern restriction.

There is no historical analogue for banning home manufacture of firearms. Quite the opposite. Before industrialized production, home manufacture and the services of local gunsmiths were how most Americans obtained firearms. The Pennsylvania rifle, the iconic firearm of the colonial frontier, was hand-built. Founding-era militias depended on privately made weapons. There were no serial numbers, no manufacturer licenses, no ATF. Every single firearm in early America would be a "ghost gun" under Virginia's new definition.

I think it is worth sitting with that for a moment. A law intended to keep dangerous weapons off the streets would, if applied to the founding era, have made felons of the men who wrote the Constitution. That's not a rhetorical point. That's a Bruen problem, and it's a serious one.

## The intent here is not subtle

Virginia's legislature moved 32 separate gun control bills through in a single session, including an AR-15 and large-capacity magazine ban. The sheer volume of legislation is itself a strategy. When you pass 32 bills at once, no advocacy organization, no gun owner, no attorney can fully track and respond to all of them before they become law. Some slip through that wouldn't have survived scrutiny on their own.

HB40 is the most dangerous of the batch precisely because it works through ignorance. It does not punish anyone for buying a gun today. It punishes someone for keeping a gun they bought yesterday, once the law changes and they never got the memo. Someone who builds a personal AR lower in 2024, stores it at home, never commits a crime, and simply does not follow the 2026 Virginia legislative session closely enough is a criminal under this law. Accidentally. Without any dangerous conduct at all.

That is a feature, not a bug. The political goal is not just to stop new ghost guns from being made. It is to create a class of retroactive felons who, once convicted, can never legally own a firearm again. One conviction strips the right permanently. That's the point.

## What the courts will likely say

Bruen-era litigation has been striking down exactly this kind of broad, historically unmoored restriction. Courts in multiple circuits have been working through challenges to regulations on frames, receivers, and home-built firearms since the Supreme Court changed the analytical framework. The Biden-era ATF rule attempting to regulate 80% lowers as firearms was [struck down in federal court](https://www.nraila.org/articles/20260315/virginia-legislature-adjourns-from-2026-session-anti-gun-bills-on-governors-desk). Virginia's version of that same policy, made into state criminal law with felony penalties, faces the same constitutional headwinds.

I think this law gets challenged and I think a Bruen analysis buries it. But that litigation takes years. In the meantime, real people in Virginia will be charged, prosecuted, and in some cases convicted. Some will lose their right to own a firearm permanently before any court strikes the law down. The damage to individuals does not get undone by a successful legal challenge five years later.

## What to do if you're in Virginia

If you own an unserialized firearm in Virginia, you have until July 1, 2027 before possession becomes a misdemeanor. Start consulting with a Second Amendment attorney now. The Virginia Citizens Defense League is tracking [all 2026 legislation](https://vcdl-lis.org/) and has been one of the most active voices pushing back on this session's bills. Governor Spanberger has until April 13 to sign or veto the bills on her desk. The window to pressure the governor's office is still open.

Beyond Virginia, watch your own state. Virginia's 2026 session is being held up as a model by gun control organizations. What they passed here is being shopped around to every state legislature with a Democratic majority. The no-grandfather-clause approach is particularly worth tracking because it is the most aggressive tool in the playbook and the least discussed.

A law that makes ordinary people into felons for conduct that was perfectly legal when they engaged in it is not gun safety. It is retroactive punishment dressed up as public health policy.
