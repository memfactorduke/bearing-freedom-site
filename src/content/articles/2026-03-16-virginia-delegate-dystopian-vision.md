## The Bottom Line

Virginia Delegate Garrett McGuire cited Liviu Librescu, a Holocaust survivor who was killed at Virginia Tech while unarmed, as evidence that people do not need guns to stop armed attackers. He got the story precisely backward. Librescu's death is one of the most powerful arguments against mandatory disarmament that exists.

## What was actually said

On March 14, 2026, during floor debate over HB 1525, a Virginia House bill raising the purchase and possession age for handguns and "assault firearms" to 21, Delegate Garrett McGuire rose to support the conference report. In doing so, he offered an argument about a provision exempting ROTC cadets from the bill's possession restrictions. His case was that you don't always need a gun to stop someone with a gun, and his supporting example was Liviu Librescu.

McGuire referenced how the Virginia Tech cadets had disarmed a shooter without firearms, and then cited Librescu specifically, praising how the professor "selflessly gave up his body" to block the classroom door and save his students.

The clip has circulated widely since the House GOP posted it to X on March 14. I want to be careful here to address what McGuire actually said rather than caricature it. The factual record of what Librescu did is not in dispute. The dispute is about what his death means, and McGuire's interpretation is, in my view, morally and logically indefensible.

## Who Liviu Librescu was

Liviu Librescu was born in Romania in 1930. He survived the Nazi occupation as a child, enduring internment in a forced labor camp and deportation to a Jewish ghetto in Focsani. After the war, he built an academic career in aeronautical engineering, eventually teaching at Tel Aviv University and then at Virginia Tech, where he was a professor of engineering science and mechanics.

On April 16, 2007, gunman Seung-Hui Cho moved through Norris Hall targeting classrooms. Librescu, 76 years old, held his classroom door shut against Cho's attempts to enter. He told his students to escape through the windows. Twenty-two of them did. Cho eventually forced his way in and shot Librescu, who died in his classroom. He was killed on Yom HaShoah, Holocaust Remembrance Day.

He was posthumously awarded the Order of the Star of Romania. He was unarmed when he died because Virginia Tech was a gun-free zone. Virginia law prohibited concealed carry on campus.

## The history McGuire invoked without understanding

The 1938 Regulations Against Jews' Possession of Weapons, enacted by the Nazi government in the days following Kristallnacht, categorically prohibited Jews from owning firearms, ammunition, or bladed weapons. The disarmament of Germany's Jewish population had been proceeding through administrative and police mechanisms since 1933, with the Gestapo directing that Jews be denied weapons permits outright by 1935. The 1938 regulation simply formalized what was already functionally complete.

Librescu was born in Romania, not Germany, and the circumstances of his specific childhood disarmament differ in detail. But the broader pattern is identical: a government stripped Jews of the ability to defend themselves as a precondition for persecution. The historical record on this is not seriously contested. Unarmed populations are easier to control and easier to kill. Authoritarian regimes have understood this for centuries.

Librescu survived the Holocaust. He survived forced labor, deportation, and the systematic murder of Jewish communities across Eastern Europe. He came to America. He taught students and published research and built a life. And he died because an institution he worked for decided that no one in his building should have the means to defend themselves against someone who did not follow the rules.

## The argument McGuire actually made

McGuire's point, stripped to its essentials, is that armed attackers can be stopped without firearms, and therefore restrictions on firearm access are acceptable. He offers Librescu as proof.

This is an argument that only works if you ignore what it cost. Librescu stopped the gunman from entering his classroom by using his body as a physical barrier. He succeeded, in the sense that most of his students escaped. He failed, in the sense that he was shot and killed. His unarmed intervention was heroic and it was also a direct consequence of the policy McGuire is defending. A man who survived Nazi disarmament and Nazi persecution came to Virginia and was disarmed again by state law, and he paid for it with his life.

The logic McGuire offers is that because Librescu's sacrifice saved students, we should perpetuate the conditions that required him to sacrifice himself. I cannot follow that reasoning. The relevant question is not "can an unarmed person sometimes stop a gunman?" The answer to that question is occasionally yes, at enormous risk. The relevant question is "should we force people into that position by prohibiting them from being armed?" To that question, Librescu's story gives an unambiguous answer.

## What self-defense actually requires

There is a recurring rhetorical pattern in gun control arguments where someone notes that armed self-defense sometimes fails, that a gun doesn't guarantee survival, that victims have been killed even while armed. All of that is true. No one on the pro-Second Amendment side claims otherwise. A firearm is a tool, not a guarantee.

But the alternative McGuire is implicitly endorsing is a world where the only option is physical resistance against a person with a firearm, and where that option requires either numerical superiority, as with the ROTC cadets who took down a school shooter, or the willingness to absorb bullets with your body, as Librescu did. Both of those are genuine options. Both of them involve people being seriously harmed or killed who might not have been if they had been armed.

I don't want force to be equal. I want the law-abiding person to have every available advantage against someone trying to kill them or their students. An elderly professor should not have to choose between his body and a door. The argument that he heroically made that choice, and that his heroism validates the policy that required it, is not an argument in favor of human dignity. It's a celebration of manufactured helplessness.

## What this moment reveals

McGuire is not an outlier in this debate. He expressed, with unusual candor, the view that underlies much of modern gun control advocacy: that citizen disarmament is acceptable because people can sometimes survive attacks without weapons, and that the cases where they cannot are simply the price of the policy. He said the quiet part out loud.

The bills he was voting for, including HB 1525, will remove firearm access from legal adults between 18 and 21 with no grandfather clause for those who already lawfully own firearms. Somewhere in Virginia right now there is a 19-year-old who bought a pistol legally and will become a criminal under this law without having done anything wrong. McGuire's justification for that is that a 76-year-old Holocaust survivor held a door shut and saved most of his students before he was killed.

In my view, that justification fails on every level. It fails historically, because Librescu's life demonstrates exactly what happens when governments decide who may and may not be armed. It fails morally, because treating a man's death as a policy success is not an argument in favor of human flourishing. It fails logically, because the existence of heroic unarmed resistance does not establish that armed resistance would have been worse.

Virginia gun owners deserve elected representatives who respect their lives enough not to use a Holocaust survivor's murder as cover for disarming them. What McGuire gave them on March 14 was the opposite.
