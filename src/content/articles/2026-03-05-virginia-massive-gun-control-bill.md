## The Bottom Line

Virginia HB40 criminalizes something Americans have done freely since before the republic existed: building their own firearms. Ten years in prison for assembling a gun without a government-assigned serial number is not a safety measure. It is a direct attack on the part of your arsenal the state cannot trace, track, or confiscate.

## What HB40 actually does

Virginia **HB40**, which cleared the General Assembly in early 2026 and is headed to Governor Abigail Spanberger's desk, bans the manufacture, possession, and transfer of unserialized firearms. That covers 3D-printed guns, 80% lowers, polymer kit builds, and any frame or receiver completed outside the licensed dealer system. First-time possession is a misdemeanor under the bill's framework. Manufacture, defined broadly enough to include assembling a kit you bought legally, is a Class 6 felony carrying up to ten years in prison. There is no grandfather clause for guns that already exist in your safe.

Let that land for a moment. You could have built a firearm last year, entirely lawfully, and under HB40 you would now be a felon for simply owning it.

## A tradition that predates the republic

The argument that self-manufactured firearms represent some dangerous modern novelty does not survive even passing contact with history. Americans have been building their own guns since the colonial era. The muskets that fired at Lexington and Concord were not manufactured by licensed dealers, serialized by a government agency, or tracked in any database. Every firearm used in the Revolutionary War, the Civil War, and for most of American history was produced without the involvement of a federal firearms licensee, because FFLs did not exist until the Gun Control Act of 1968.

The Duke Center for Firearms Law has noted that under the *Bruen* framework the Supreme Court established in 2022, the text, history, and tradition of the Second Amendment is controlling. And that tradition is unambiguous: Americans have always built their own arms. There is no historical analog for criminalizing the personal manufacture of a firearm for lawful use. Virginia is not operating within the constitutional tradition. It is openly defying it.

## The serialization agenda

The gun control advocacy line on this legislation is that unserialized firearms impede law enforcement tracing. That argument is, at best, incomplete. The overwhelming majority of firearms used in crimes are not DIY builds. They come through theft, straw purchases, and illegal secondary transfers. A person with the technical knowledge to complete an 80% lower, run a 3D printer successfully, and assemble a functional firearm is not the profile of the typical perpetrator in a violent crime. The premise that ghost guns represent a unique public safety threat is not supported by crime data.

What is unique about unserialized firearms is that the government does not know who has them.

That distinction matters enormously. The ATF has accumulated hundreds of millions of out-of-business FFL records at its National Tracing Center. As of late 2021, the bureau held nearly 921 million such records, with that number growing by millions every month as dealers close and transfer their files. When a licensed dealer sells a firearm, the Form 4473 creates a record. When that dealer later goes out of business, that record goes to the ATF. The agency is legally prohibited from building a searchable registry, but the practical effect of holding nearly a billion transaction records in a digitized repository is that the government has a very detailed picture of who bought what through licensed channels.

A firearm you built yourself does not appear in that picture. Virginia's legislators know this. The purpose of HB40 is not to prevent crime. It is to close the one gap in the state's ability to account for every firearm in private hands.

## The bigger pattern in Virginia

HB40 does not stand alone. It is one piece of a coordinated legislative push that produced more than twenty gun control bills in the 2026 session. **HB702**, which also advanced to Spanberger's desk, mandates that every locality in Virginia establish a firearm buyback or give-back program by January 1, 2028. Law enforcement agencies must designate times and locations for voluntary surrenders, submit annual reports on firearms collected, and destroy any gun not flagged for a crime investigation.

The sequence is worth stating plainly. First, serialize and track every firearm that passes through licensed channels. Second, criminalize the manufacture and possession of any firearm that bypasses that tracking system. Third, require every local police department to operate an infrastructure for collecting firearms from the public.

I am not in the habit of assuming the worst possible motive when a more charitable explanation is available. But there is no charitable explanation available here. Each of these laws is individually explicable as a regulation. Together, they describe a strategy for disarmament. Virginia is building the scaffolding.

## What the Senate margin means

The Virginia Senate broke 21 Democrats to 19 Republicans in the 2026 session. That two-vote margin is the entire reason this package exists. Every single one of these bills, the ghost gun ban, the buyback mandate, the magazine limits, the carry restrictions, passed because Democrats hold a slim majority in one chamber of one state legislature. Two seats. That is the distance between a functioning Second Amendment in Virginia and a criminal record for building a gun in your garage.

In my view, that fact deserves more attention than it usually gets in national coverage of these issues. People vote in presidential elections and tune out the down-ballot races where Second Amendment policy is actually being made. Virginia is demonstrating in real time what that inattention costs.

## Constitutional exposure

I do think HB40 faces a serious constitutional challenge under *Bruen*. The Supreme Court's text, history, and tradition standard is precisely the analysis that exposes this bill. There is no founding-era analog for prohibiting personal firearm manufacture, because the founders would have found the concept incomprehensible. Courts applying *Bruen* faithfully should not be able to sustain HB40.

But litigation takes time. The bill's penalties are real now, for real people, today. Someone who built a legal firearm before this law passes is now holding a potential felony. The chilling effect kicks in before a single case reaches a federal court. That is part of the design. You do not need to win enforcement. You just need to make gun owners uncertain whether their otherwise lawful conduct is a crime. Uncertainty alone produces compliance.

Virginia is not done. The legislative session may have closed, but Spanberger has signaled she is open to further restrictions, including an assault weapons ban modeled on other states' laws. What is happening in Richmond is a template. Other state legislatures with Democratic majorities are watching it work in real time. If you care about the Second Amendment, watch Virginia. This is where the fight is.
