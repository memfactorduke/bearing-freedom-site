## The Bottom Line

The Department of Justice under Harmeet Dhillon is the most pro-Second Amendment federal law enforcement apparatus in living memory, and it is actively preparing to challenge Virginia's gun laws. That is genuinely good news. It does not mean Virginia gun owners will be shielded from harm while the litigation plays out.

## A DOJ that actually understands the Second Amendment

For the better part of two decades, the Department of Justice treated the Second Amendment as an obstacle. When the Supreme Court issued **Heller** in 2008 and established that the right to keep and bear arms is individual, the executive branch grudgingly accepted the ruling while finding every possible avenue to limit its reach. Under Obama and Biden, DOJ litigators defended magazine bans, assault weapons bans, and a licensing apparatus in DC so burdensome that it functioned as a de facto prohibition. The department was an adversary, and gun owners knew it.

That has changed in a meaningful way. Harmeet Dhillon, heading the DOJ's Civil Rights Division, has created what amounts to a dedicated Second Amendment enforcement shop. The division is hiring attorneys specifically trained in constitutional gun rights litigation. It has already filed a suit against DC challenging its semi-automatic rifle ban, filed in the 7th Circuit challenging Illinois' assault weapons ban as unconstitutional under **Bruen**, and sued the Virgin Islands over its unconstitutional permitting process. The message Dhillon is sending to state capitals is not ambiguous: if you pass gun laws that conflict with Supreme Court precedent, the federal government will sue you.

She has been explicit about Virginia. When Virginia's magazine restriction was moving through the legislature, Dhillon posted publicly that it was "a blatant violation of Supreme Court precedent and it will not stand." That is not a boilerplate press release from a press secretary. That is the head of the Civil Rights Division signaling prosecutorial intent before the ink is dry.

## Why the DOJ hasn't sued Virginia yet

A number of frustrated gun owners have asked why the federal government isn't already in court stopping this. The answer is procedural, and it matters: you cannot obtain a federal injunction against a law that does not yet exist. Most of Virginia's 2026 gun control package, including SB749's assault weapons ban, has an operative date of July 1, 2026. Until that date, there is no ongoing constitutional violation to enjoin. Courts require an actual case or controversy. You cannot preemptively challenge a law that hasn't taken effect.

The moment these bills become effective and enforceable, the legal landscape changes. At that point the DOJ can, and almost certainly will, file for injunctive relief in federal court. The combination of DOJ litigation, NRA-ILA suits, GOA challenges, and potentially SAF actions means Virginia will face a multi-front legal assault. Some of those challenges will be stronger than others. Some will produce early temporary restraining orders. Others will grind through the courts for years.

## The honest account of what federal litigation can and cannot do

I want to be direct about something that gets glossed over in this conversation, because optimism that papers over real consequences does a disservice to the people affected.

Even in the best-case scenario, federal litigation takes time. The average civil rights case from filing to a circuit court opinion runs two to four years. If the 4th Circuit upholds Virginia's laws, as it has upheld similar laws in Maryland, the Supreme Court then has to decide whether to grant certiorari. That process could take the better part of a decade, and there is no guarantee the Court takes the case. In the meantime, Virginia's laws are in effect and being enforced.

That means innocent people will be caught up in this. A gun owner who bought a perfectly legal firearm two years ago and didn't track Virginia's 2026 legislative session closely enough to know it was now covered by SB749 is at real legal risk. The penalty structure, a Class 1 misdemeanor carrying up to 12 months and a $2,500 fine per violation plus a three-year firearms prohibition, will fall on people who did nothing wrong. Some will have their gun rights stripped for years based on possession of a firearm they lawfully purchased and never imagined would be restricted. That is not a hypothetical outcome. It is a predictable one.

## The broader contradiction Virginia isn't being asked to explain

There is something worth sitting with in the legislative record from Virginia's 2026 session. In the same session that produced the assault weapons ban, the magazine restrictions, the gutting of concealed carry reciprocity, and the ghost gun ban, Virginia's Democratic majority also softened mandatory minimum sentencing for violent offenders and advanced legislation reducing penalties for certain crimes involving actual dangerous conduct. The pattern is exact: less accountability for people who commit violence, more accountability for people who own legal property.

This inversion ought to provoke serious questions from anyone who accepts the stated public safety rationale for gun control. If the goal is reducing violence, you do not simultaneously reduce penalties for the people committing it while criminalizing the possession of a barrel shroud by a law-abiding citizen. These two policy directions are not accidentally in tension. They reflect a coherent set of priorities that simply are not organized around public safety.

I think the Second Amendment community is sometimes reluctant to make this argument forcefully because it sounds like deflection. But it is not deflection. It is the most direct possible critique of the underlying logic. Virginia Democrats are not making a public safety argument in good faith. They are making a political argument about who should and should not be armed.

## Why elections are still the only durable solution

The DOJ under Dhillon is doing real work. The litigation being filed is not performative. The Second Amendment enforcement section within the Civil Rights Division represents a structural shift in how federal power is being deployed on gun rights, and I think it deserves genuine appreciation from people who have watched the department function as an adversary for years.

But Dhillon herself has noted that her tenure has a defined timeline. Whatever this administration accomplishes in the next two-plus years can be undone by the next one. DOJ priorities change with administrations. The Second Amendment enforcement section that is being built today can be quietly defunded and repurposed the next time power changes hands. Courts can be stacked. Precedents can be narrowed or ignored. Federal intervention is a useful near-term tool, not a permanent structural protection.

The only durable protection for gun rights in Virginia is political accountability. Virginia lost this fight because of one election: Glenn Youngkin's successful vetoes of virtually identical legislation kept these laws off the books for years, and when he was replaced by a governor who supports them, the dam broke. The 2026 legislative session sent roughly 25 gun control bills to Spanberger's desk. That didn't happen because of some abstract shift in public opinion. It happened because the people who vote in Virginia elections sent enough legislators to Richmond who want this outcome.

You can litigate your way to temporary relief. You cannot litigate your way to a legislature that respects individual rights. That requires showing up, which is the less satisfying but more permanent answer.

## The signal Dhillon is sending

Whatever the eventual outcomes in court, the signal that the DOJ under Dhillon is sending to state attorneys general and governors matters in its own right. State officials considering similar legislation now face the prospect of direct federal litigation, not just private lawsuits from gun rights organizations. That changes the calculus for at least some officials in at least some states. A governor in a purple state who might otherwise be comfortable signing a magazine ban has to weigh whether they want a DOJ lawsuit as a political liability.

That deterrence effect is real and it is valuable. The question for Virginia gun owners right now is how to operate during the period between when these laws take effect and when courts resolve their constitutionality. My strong advice is to consult an attorney familiar with Virginia firearms law before July 1. The legal landscape is about to change materially, and the people who will suffer for being uninformed are not the politicians who voted for this. They are you.
